How to Check if a Link Is Safe — Before You Click

Updated June 2026 · 4 min read

One wrong click is all it takes. A fake "your parcel is on hold" text, a too-good-to-be-true sale, a login page that looks exactly like your bank's — almost every online scam starts with a single link. The good news? You don't need to be a tech expert to stay safe. Thirty seconds of checking before you click beats hours of cleaning up afterwards. Here are six simple habits that work on any phone or computer.

1. Hover (desktop) or long-press (mobile) to preview the real URL

The words you see in a link and the place it actually takes you can be completely different. A message can display www.yourbank.com while the link underneath points somewhere else entirely.

  • On a computer: rest your mouse pointer on the link without clicking. The true address appears in the bottom corner of the browser window.
  • On a phone: press and hold the link for a second. A small preview pops up showing where the link really goes, usually with options like "Copy link". Keep your finger down — a quick tap will open it.

If the address you see doesn't match what the message claims, you already have your answer: don't click.

2. Read the domain right-to-left

The domain is the part of a web address between https:// and the first / — basically, the site's name. Here's the trick scammers hope you never learn: only the part just before the ending (.com, .in, .org and so on) tells you who really owns the site. So read it right-to-left.

  • paypal.com — owned by PayPal.
  • accounts.paypal.com — still PayPal; "accounts" is just a section of their site.
  • paypal.com.example-verify.example — not PayPal. Reading right-to-left, the real owner is example-verify.example; the "paypal.com" at the front is just a costume.

Anyone can stick any word on the left side of a domain they own. The rightmost part before the ending is the only part that has to be genuine.

3. Beware shortened links

Shortened links — the tiny ones like bit.ly/xyz123 — exist to make long addresses fit in a text message. That's also exactly why scammers love them: they hide the destination completely, so none of the checks above work. From safest choice to last resort:

  1. Don't click at all. If the message claims to be from your bank, courier, or electricity provider, go to their official app or website directly instead.
  2. Expand it first. A link expander is a free website where you paste a short link and it shows you the full destination without visiting it. Search for "link expander" and pick one you like.
  3. If you do click, check the address bar carefully on whatever page loads — and never type passwords or card details there.

4. Check for look-alike spellings

When scammers can't use a real name, they register one that looks close enough to fool a quick glance. Watch for:

  • Digits posing as letters: paypa1.example — that last character is the number 1, not the letter L.
  • One-letter swaps or extras: paypall.example or paypol.example.
  • Letter pairs that blur together: an "r" next to an "n" can pass for an "m" when you're reading fast.

The fix is simple: slow down and read the domain letter by letter. Better still, open the genuine site in another tab and compare the two addresses side by side.

5. When in doubt, type the address yourself

The strongest move is also the easiest: skip the link entirely. If a message says your account will be blocked, your KYC (the identity proof banks ask for) needs updating, or a parcel is stuck and needs a small fee paid by UPI (India's instant payment system) or card — don't tap the link to "fix" it. Open your bank's official app, or type the website address you already know straight into the browser. If the alert is real, it will be waiting for you there. If everything looks normal when you log in the usual way, you've just side-stepped a scam. Genuine organisations never mind you coming in through the front door.

6. Let a tool watch your back

Even careful people get tired, and a scammer only needs to catch you once. So stack the odds in your favour:

  • Keep your browser's built-in protection on. Chrome, Edge, Firefox and Safari all warn you about known dangerous sites — and the warnings work best when the browser is kept up to date.
  • Add an automatic checker. Our free extension quietly rates every page you open and turns its shield red the moment something looks dangerous — useful for the links you click before your brain catches up.
  • Trust the warning. If your browser or a security tool says a page is risky, close the tab. No discount or delivery is worth ignoring it.

None of these checks takes longer than a deep breath, and together they stop the vast majority of link-based tricks. Make them a habit, and the next suspicious link in your inbox becomes just another deleted message.

The 10-second link check: hover or long-press to see the real address; read the domain right-to-left; never trust a shortened link from a stranger; spell-check the name letter by letter; and when in doubt, don't click — type the official address yourself instead.
