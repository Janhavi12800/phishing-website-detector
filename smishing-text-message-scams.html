Smishing: How to Spot Text Message Scams (Parcel, Bank-KYC, Job Offers)

Updated June 2026 · 4 min read

Smishing is phishing done over text message — the word squashes together “SMS” and “phishing”, and it simply means a scam text built to make you tap a dangerous link or hand over money. Texts feel more personal than email, they land on the device you check fifty times a day, and there is no spam folder catching the worst ones. That is exactly why scammers love them. Here are the four smishing scripts you are most likely to meet, and a simple routine for checking any message safely.

The fake parcel or delivery text

The message says a package is waiting for you, but something small has gone wrong: an “incomplete address”, a customs charge, or a tiny redelivery fee.

“Your package is on hold at our warehouse. Pay the redelivery fee of ₹25 within 24 hours: courier-track.example.com”

Tap the link and you land on a polished copy of a real courier website — right logo, right colours — that exists only to collect your card details. The “small fee” is the bait; once you type your full card number, the scammer can attempt much bigger charges. India has seen huge waves of fake India Post and courier texts, but the same template runs worldwide using whichever delivery brand is popular locally.

The tell: real couriers do not collect fees through a random link in a text, and they do not threaten to return or destroy your parcel within hours.

The bank “KYC expiry” scare

KYC stands for “Know Your Customer” — the routine identity check where a bank confirms who you are using your documents. Scammers love the term because it sounds official and frightening:

“Dear customer, your bank KYC has expired. Your account will be SUSPENDED today. Update now: mybank-kyc-update.example.com”

The link asks for your net-banking password, card number, or UPI PIN (UPI is India’s instant bank-payment system; the PIN approves money going out, never coming in). Some versions instead ask you to call a number or install a “support app” that lets the scammer see your screen while you log in.

The tell: banks handle KYC inside their official app or at a branch, and no genuine bank suspends your account “today” via a text-message link.

Too-good job offers on SMS and WhatsApp

“Earn ₹5,000 per day, part time, work from home! Just like videos and rate products.” These usually arrive from unknown numbers, often with a friendly “HR manager” waiting on WhatsApp or Telegram. The scam runs in two stages:

  1. You complete a few easy tasks and actually receive small payments — this builds trust.
  2. You are asked to deposit your own money to “unlock premium tasks” with bigger returns. The deposit vanishes, and so does the recruiter.

The tell: no real employer recruits strangers by bulk text, and no real job ever asks you to pay money in order to earn money.

The electricity-bill disconnection threat

“Your electricity will be disconnected tonight at 9:30 PM as your previous bill was not updated. Contact our officer immediately.”

The oddly specific evening deadline is designed to panic you when offices are closed and you cannot easily check. The “officer” is a personal mobile number that walks you into a fake payment link or a screen-sharing app. Real power companies send notices that quote your consumer number, give days of warning, and use official channels — not a stranger’s phone at night.

How to verify any message safely

  1. Never tap the link in the message. This one habit defeats almost every smishing attempt.
  2. Go in through the front door. Open the company’s official app, or type its website address yourself. If your parcel, account, or bill genuinely has a problem, it will show up there.
  3. Check the sender. A “bank alert” from a random ten-digit personal number is a giveaway — though official-looking sender names can be faked too, so never rely on this alone.
  4. Watch for the fee. Real companies do not ask for payments, OTPs, or PINs through unexpected links. Any message mixing urgency with money is a scam until proven otherwise.

Here is the difference side by side:

  • Real: “Your order #4471 was delivered today. Thank you for shopping with us.” — matches an order you actually placed, asks for nothing.
  • Fake: “Pay ₹25 within 2 hours or your parcel will be returned: parcel-fee.example.com” — money, a deadline, and a strange link.

If a text asks you to tap a link to pay, verify, or “update” anything — don’t. Open the official app or type the company’s address yourself; a real problem will still be there.

What to do if you already tapped

Tapping the link alone is rarely a disaster. What matters is what you typed or installed afterwards.

  • Entered a password? Change it immediately, plus any other account where you reuse it.
  • Entered card or bank details? Call your bank on the official number printed on the back of your card and block the card. Speed matters more than embarrassment.
  • Shared an OTP or UPI PIN? Tell your bank right away and report the fraud to your country’s official cybercrime helpline or portal.
  • Installed an app? Uninstall it, restart the phone, then change your important passwords from a different device.
  • Finally, block the sender, report the message as spam, and delete it so you never tap it again by accident.

Scam texts work on speed and panic. Give every unexpected message thirty seconds of calm, and smishing loses almost all of its power.
