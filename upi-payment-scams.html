7 Common UPI Scams — and How to Stay Safe

Updated June 2026 · 4 min read

UPI — short for Unified Payments Interface — is India's instant payment system: link your bank account to an app like Google Pay, PhonePe or Paytm, and you can pay anyone in seconds using just a phone number or a QR code. It's brilliant. It's also exactly what scammers love, because money sent by UPI arrives instantly and is very hard to get back. Here are the seven most common UPI scams, and the simple habits that defeat every one of them.

1. The "request money" trick

UPI doesn't just let people send you money — it also lets them request money from you. Scammers abuse this constantly. A typical setup: you're selling a sofa online, the "buyer" agrees instantly, then says, "I've transferred the amount — just approve the notification and enter your PIN to receive it."

That notification is not a payment coming in. It's a payment request going out. Approve it, enter your PIN, and your money leaves your account.

The defence is one simple fact: receiving money on UPI never requires you to approve anything, tap anything or enter your PIN. Money genuinely sent to you simply appears.

Your UPI PIN is only ever used to send money. Anyone who asks you to enter it to receive money is scamming you — no exceptions, ever.

2. Fake cashback and reward links

"Congratulations! You've won ₹4,999 cashback — claim before midnight!" The message arrives by SMS or WhatsApp and links to something like upi-cashback-claim.example.com. Tapping it either opens a fake page that harvests your details, or fires off a money request dressed up as a reward.

Remember how real cashback works: it's credited automatically. There's no claiming step, no countdown timer and definitely no PIN. A "reward" that needs your PIN is a withdrawal in disguise.

3. The fake "payment successful" screenshot

This one targets sellers. A buyer "pays" for your listing and sends a screenshot showing Payment Successful — ₹15,000. Screenshots are trivially easy to edit or generate, and fraudsters keep convincing fakes ready to go.

Never hand over goods because of a picture. Open your own banking or UPI app and confirm the money has actually been credited — your balance is the only proof that counts.

4. QR code swaps — the "scan to receive" lie

Memorise this: scanning a QR code is only ever for paying. There is no such thing as "scan this code to receive money", yet scammers say it daily — usually to people selling things or expecting refunds. You scan, enter your PIN to "confirm receipt", and pay them instead.

A related trick hits shops: criminals paste their own QR sticker over a merchant's real one. After scanning, check that the name on your screen matches the business before you confirm.

5. Fake customer-care numbers in search results

Your refund is stuck, so you search for the app's "customer care number" and call the first result. Scammers plant fake numbers in ads, bogus business listings and forum posts precisely so worried people will call them. The friendly "agent" then walks you through "verification" steps that are really transfers out of your account.

Get support contacts only from inside the official app or the company's official website — never from a search result or a number someone messages you. A browser tool like our free Spam & Phishing Site Detector extension can also flag lookalike "support" pages before you trust them.

6. "I transferred money by mistake — please return it"

A stranger calls, sounding panicked: "I sent ₹10,000 to your number by mistake — please send it back." Sometimes a fake SMS styled like a bank credit alert lands at the same moment. Either no money ever arrived and you'd be sending your own, or stolen money really did arrive and "returning" it makes you a link in a laundering chain.

Check your actual balance in your own app — not the SMS. If money truly arrived, don't send anything yourself: tell the caller to go through their bank, and inform yours. Banks can reverse genuine mistakes properly.

7. The remote-access app trick

Someone claiming to be from your bank or payment app reports a "problem with your account" and asks you to install a support tool such as AnyDesk or TeamViewer. These are screen-sharing apps — legitimate software that lets another person see, and sometimes control, your phone. Once it's running, they ask you to open your bank app "to check the issue" and quietly read your balance, PIN and incoming one-time passwords.

No bank and no payment company will ever ask you to install a screen-sharing app. The moment you hear those words, hang up.

The golden rules of UPI safety

Every scam above falls apart against a few habits:

  • Your PIN is only for sending. Receiving money never needs a PIN, an approval or a scan.
  • Scanning a QR code means paying. Always. "Scan to receive" is a lie.
  • Banks never call asking you to approve a request, scan a code or install an app. Real problems aren't fixed through surprise phone calls.
  • Verify customer-care numbers from the official app only — never from a search engine, an ad or a forwarded message.
  • Trust your own app, not screenshots or SMS, before treating any payment as received.
  • Slow down. Countdown timers, panicked callers and "account blocked" warnings are pressure tactics. Anything genuine will still be genuine in ten minutes.

If you do get caught, act fast: use your bank's official helpline to block further payments, and report the fraud to your national cybercrime portal. Speed is the scammer's weapon — make it yours too.
